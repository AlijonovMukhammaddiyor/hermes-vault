# Briefing — 2026-07-23

> [!star] Today's must-reads

> - **[OpenAI drops a big Tuesday: Presence (enterprise agent platform), Effingham data center, + DOE science partnership](https://openai.com/index/introducing-openai-presence)** — OpenAI enters the enterprise agent platform market directly · Presence is a platform for deploying trusted voice and chat agents for customer and internal workflows. NTT DATA already has it running at 9,000-employee scale, cutting incident analysis to 30 minutes. Separately: Project Camellia in Georgia (data center expansion), and a commitment to work with US national labs on AI-for-science. This is OpenAI's biggest non-model announcement day in months — they're building the infrastructure and platform to make agents a business, not just a research demo.
>
> - **[Are AI Labs Pelicanmaxxing?](https://dylancastillo.co/posts/pelicanmaxxing.html)** (401pts HN) — the best AI benchmarking critique this year · Pelicans swallow anything. The metaphor: AI labs are "pelicanmaxxing" — ingesting every benchmark, every test, every eval until the model becomes indistinguishable from the thing being measured. Training data contamination, benchmark overfitting, and Goodhart's Law at frontier-lab scale. Genuinely important for anyone who reads model release blog posts critically. This is what good technical skepticism looks like.
>
> - **[GigaToken: ~1000x faster language model tokenization](https://github.com/marcelroed/gigatoken/)** (374pts HN) — open source, and potentially a big deal for inference economics · If tokenization is a meaningful fraction of serving cost and this actually delivers at scale, the implications for real-time agentic workloads are significant. Worth tracking even if the 1000x claim needs independent verification.
>
> - **[John C. Dvorak has died](https://twitter.com/na_announce/status/2079952538040672302)** (561pts HN) — one of tech journalism's most irreverent voices is gone · For anyone who grew up reading PC Magazine in its heyday, Dvorak defined a certain skeptical, independent, no-bullshit tech commentary that barely exists anymore. A generation of tech writers learned from him — or learned to argue with him. He made tech commentary fun.
>
> - **[Everyone Should Know SIMD](https://mitchellh.com/writing/everyone-should-know-simd)** (258pts HN) — Mitchell Hashimoto on why understanding the hardware still matters · A deep, practical guide to SIMD programming from the HashiCorp founder. In an era where everyone reaches for AI to solve performance problems, this is a reminder that understanding what the CPU actually does is still the difference between good and great engineering. The fundamentals don't go obsolete.
>
> - **[The startup's Postgres survival guide](https://hatchet.run/blog/postgres-survival-guide)** (321pts HN) — practical, hard-won advice on not dying to your database · Connection pooling, safe migrations, indexing strategy, monitoring — the stuff that kills startups who treat Postgres as a black box. Written by people who learned the hard way. If you're running anything in production on Postgres and you haven't thought about these things, today's the day.
>
> - **[Making](https://beej.us/blog/data/ai-making/)** (280pts HN) — a philosophical essay on why we still make things when AI can generate anything · Beej (of Beej's Guide to Network Programming fame) writes about the joy of the act itself. In a world where AI can produce a painting, a poem, or a program in seconds, why do we still sit down and do the work? Because the making is the point. Beautifully argued, and the kind of thing the HN front page rarely surfaces anymore.
>
> - **[So Reddit has decided that plain HTML is unsafe](https://www.cole-k.com/2026/07/21/reddit/)** (286pts HN) — a milestone in the enclosure of the open web · Reddit now redirects plain HTML pages as "unsafe," pushing users to their app. It's a small UI change with big implications: the open web is steadily being replaced by platform-walled gardens, and the justifications are getting thinner. The comments thread is worth reading too.
>
> - **[I Inspected My Take-Home Interview Project. It Was a Whole Operation](https://citizendot.github.io/articles/fake-job-interview-git-hook-malware/)** (270pts HN) — the job interview as attack vector · A developer received a take-home project from what looked like a legitimate company. The git hooks were malware. The whole thing was an elaborate operation to exfiltrate data. Equal parts terrifying and fascinating — and a reminder that in 2026, even your interview process needs a threat model.
>
> - **[RefluXFS: Linux Kernel Local Privilege Escalation to Root in XFS (CVE-2026-64600)](https://blog.qualys.com/vulnerabilities-threat-research/2026/07/22/refluxfs-a-linux-kernel-local-privilege-escalation-to-root-in-xfs-cve-2026-64600)** (Lobsters) — Qualys found an LPE in the XFS filesystem · XFS is the default filesystem on RHEL and widely deployed. Local privilege escalation means: if an attacker has any code execution on the box, they can get root. Patch it.
>
> - **[ByteByteGo: Best Practices for Building AI Agents That Work in Production](https://blog.bytebytego.com/p/best-practices-for-building-ai-agents)** — battle-tested patterns for production agents · Distills collective thinking from real deployments into practical guidance. Agents in production are still hard; this is one of the better structured overviews of what actually works.
>
> - **[Simon Willison: Orchestrions](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/22/all-the-orchestrions/)** — agent orchestration patterns cataloged · Simon maps the emerging taxonomy of how agents are composed, controlled, and chained. Always worth reading because he tests things before opining, which is rarer than it should be.
