# Briefing — 2026-07-19

> [!star] Today's must-reads

> - **[GPT-5.6 used a prompt to close a 30-year gap in convex optimization](https://old.reddit.com/r/math/comments/1uxj3cy/after_openais_cdc_proof_announcement_gpt56_used_a/)** (504pts HN) — an AI proved a theorem that stumped mathematicians for three decades · The prompt engineering details matter less than the signal: frontier models are now producing genuine mathematical breakthroughs, not just pattern-matching. This isn't "AI helps code" — it's AI extending human knowledge in pure math. The CDC (Computational Discovery) program at OpenAI is crossing from assistant to co-author.

> - **[The Kimi K3 Moment](https://stephen.bochinski.dev/blog/2026/07/18/the-kimi-k3-moment/)** (295pts HN) — 2.8T-A50B parameters, the largest open model ever released, Opus 4.8-class at Sonnet 5 pricing · Kimi K3 just leapfrogged the open-weight field. When a model this large ships open, the economics of self-hosting shift. Combined with Kimi K3 topping Arena's coding leaderboard, the "open models are always behind" narrative takes a real hit. This is the story Nathan Lambert previewed in "6 months to live for open models" — and it's accelerating.

> - **[What AI did to StackOverflow in a graph](https://data.stackexchange.com/stackoverflow/query/1953768#graph)** (365pts HN) — the data tells the story: traffic, questions, answers — all in visible decline · This isn't anecdote anymore. The graph quantifies what every developer feels: the center of gravity for technical Q&A has shifted. StackOverflow isn't dying — it's being unbundled. Code questions go to agents, architecture questions go to blogs, everything else goes to Discord. The canonical Q&A format that defined a generation of developers is being replaced by something more fragmented but arguably more useful.

> - **[Fable 5 vs. GPT-5.6 Sol on an NP-Hard Problem: Does /goal help?](https://charlesazam.com/blog/fable-5-gpt-5-6-sol-goal/)** (214pts HN) — a direct head-to-head comparison of the two frontier models on genuinely hard problems · Rigorous, reproducible, and fair. The `/goal` prompting technique (giving the model a high-level objective rather than step-by-step instructions) shows measurable gains — but not on everything. This is the kind of empirical model comparison we need more of, and less vibes-based benchmarking.

> - **[OpenAI: A scorecard for the AI age](https://openai.com/index/a-scorecard-for-the-ai-age)** (OpenAI, Jul 17) — CFO Sarah Friar introduces a practical framework: useful work, cost per successful task, dependability, return on compute · After a year of "AI will change everything," someone finally wrote the spreadsheet. This is the piece to send to your CFO/CTO when they ask "but what's the ROI?" Four concrete metrics, no hand-waving. Friar is doing what every enterprise AI buyer has been begging for: giving them a language to talk about value.

> - **[Martin Fowler: The Archaeologist's Copilot](https://martinfowler.com/articles/archaeologist-copilot.html)** (Jul 16) — using AI for legacy modernization, grounded in evidence not vibes · The key insight: AI gave plausible answers that didn't hold up in the actual codebase. Progress came only when the process was constrained by evidence — stable Docker environments, tests, gradual refactoring. If your team is using AI to modernize old systems, this is the playbook. The "AI is most useful when constrained by evidence" lesson generalizes far beyond legacy code.

> - **[MCP vs A2A vs ACP: How AI Agents Actually Talk to Each Other](https://blog.bytebytego.com/p/mcp-vs-a2a-vs-acp-how-ai-agents-actually)** (ByteByteGo, Jul 18) — the agent interoperability protocol landscape, clearly explained · MCP (Anthropic's Model Context Protocol), A2A (Google's Agent-to-Agent), and ACP (Agent Communication Protocol) each solve different parts of the same problem. If you're building multi-agent systems or tool-using agents, this is the map you need. The protocol wars are here, and they matter for the same reason HTTP vs. gRPC vs. WebSocket mattered.

> - **[Reviewing AI Code Is Not A Viable Argument](https://softwaremaxims.com/blog/reviewing-ai-code)** (Lobsters, Jul 18) — a provocative take on the code review bottleneck debate · The argument: if AI generates code faster than humans can meaningfully review it, "just review the AI's output" isn't a sustainable position. The piece challenges the comforting assumption that human judgment remains the ultimate gate. Whether you agree or not, the framing sharpens the debate — is code review the right abstraction in the agent era, or are we defending a process that's already obsolete?

> - **[Speech Recognition and TTS in less than 500kb](https://github.com/moonshine-ai/moonshine/tree/main/micro)** (277pts HN) — Moonshine micro: production-quality speech models that fit in half a megabyte · This is genuinely impressive engineering. In an era where models are measured in hundreds of billions of parameters, someone shipped speech-to-text and text-to-speech at a size where the entire model fits in L2 cache. The constraint-driven design here teaches more about model architecture than most papers.

> - **[The bottleneck for AI agents isn't the model anymore. It's the context layer](https://thenewstack.io/ai-agent-infrastructure-bottleneck/)** (The New Stack, Jul 18) — the infrastructure argument shifts from model quality to context engineering · Two years of watching agent teams: they upgrade models, hit the same reliability ceilings, upgrade again. The pattern is now clear — the bottleneck is context management, retrieval, and structuring, not model capability. This aligns with what Martin Fowler's Fragments noted: context windows are big, but models don't focus on the right bits. The teams winning at agent reliability are the ones treating context as a first-class engineering problem.

> - **[Martin Fowler: DSLs Enable Reliable Use of LLMs](https://martinfowler.com/articles/llm-and-dsls.html)** (Jul 14) — domain-specific languages as the harness that makes LLM-generated code reliable · Unmesh Joshi's Tickloom example shows the pattern: use an LLM as a partner to iteratively build a DSL, then use the DSL as the LLM's constrained output format. The DSL becomes the source of truth. This is the most elegant articulation yet of "harness engineering" — not guardrails after the fact, but a constrained language that makes wrong output structurally impossible.
